The 20th Century · The Avant-Garde Era

The Vanguards

When the camera changed everything — and artists had to reinvent what art could be.

The Revolution

When the Camera Arrived

Imagine being Leonardo da Vinci, telling La Gioconda: “I can preserve your face forever, make you look so real you seem alive.” It took him weeks of patient sittings. Then, three centuries later — the photograph. One click. Done.

The camera didn’t just copy portraits — it destroyed one of painting’s core purposes. Suddenly, artists had to ask: if a machine can capture reality perfectly, what is art even for?

Portrait of Philip IV

Diego Velázquez · c. 1655 · Painted over weeks

Only a master painter could preserve your face for posterity. A privilege of royalty and the wealthy.

Emir Alim Khan of Bukhara

Photograph · 1911 · One instant

By 1911, anyone could have a photographic portrait in an instant. The exclusive power of painting was gone forever.

The painters of the 20th century didn’t disappear — they reinvented themselves. If a camera could capture reality, art had to explore something the camera never could. That’s where the Avant-Garde movements were born.

Movement I

Expressionism: Paint What You Feel

Can a camera photograph a panic attack? Can it capture the feeling of your heart racing, the world warping around you? No. But Edvard Munch could.

The Scream is not a portrait of a person. It’s a portrait of an emotion. Munch suffered anxiety attacks, and he painted exactly what they felt like: a figure dissolving, a sky turning blood-red, the landscape twisting as if the world itself were screaming.

“I was walking with friends. The sun began to set — suddenly the sky turned blood red. I felt an infinite scream passing through nature.” — Edvard Munch, journal entry

The Scream

Edvard Munch · 1893 · National Museum, Oslo

The Expressionist Principle

Expressionists distort reality to make it express feelings. Curves, twisted proportions, unnatural colors — everything serves the emotion, not the photograph.

You might have seen The Scream’s iconic face used in the horror movie Scream. That’s how powerful the image is: it lives in our collective imagination more than a century later.

Movement II

Abstract Art: Beyond the Visible

Composition 7

Wassily Kandinsky · 1913

When Kandinsky first saw Monet’s haystacks, he was confused — he couldn’t tell what was painted. But the canvas moved him deeply. He realized: color and composition alone could carry emotion, without needing to represent anything recognizable.

Abstract art doesn’t represent “nothing.” It represents a deeper layer of reality: feelings, rhythms, energies. Like music — you don’t see a story in a symphony, yet it moves you profoundly.

Kandinsky even described his paintings like musical compositions — hence titles like “Composition 7.” He believed colors had their own inner sound.

Piet Mondrian takes abstraction even further. His Composition in Red, Blue and Yellow is pure geometry: black lines, three primary colors, white space. Nothing else.

Is it “nothing”? Or is it the essence of balance, structure, harmony — the same ideals Greek architects pursued in the Parthenon, now stripped to their mathematical core?

Next time you see a Mondrian, don’t ask “what is it.” Ask: “what does it make me feel?”

Composition II in Red, Blue and Yellow

Piet Mondrian · 1930 · Kunsthaus Zürich

Movement III

Surrealism: Dreams on Canvas

The Persistence of Memory

Salvador Dalí · 1931 · MoMA, New York

Melting clocks draped over a landscape. A figure dissolving into the earth. A strange, dreamlike silence. This is Dalí’s most famous painting— and it asks a radical question: is time really as rigid as we think?

The clock was invented to make time objective and fixed — everyone arriving at the same train station at the exact same moment. But in our lived experience, time feels elastic. An hour of boredom lasts forever. A wonderful summer disappears in a flash.

Dalí paints the subjective truth of time — something no photograph, no clock, no calendar can ever capture.

The Man with the Hidden Face

René Magritte’s self-portrait shows a man in a suit — but a green apple floats in front of his face, hiding it completely. You can see his eyes behind the fruit, watching you.

Surrealism was deeply influenced by Freud’s psychoanalysis — the idea that beneath our conscious mind lives a vast, strange unconscious world full of desires, fears, and memories we don’t fully understand.

What is Magritte hiding behind that apple? His true self. His unconscious. The part of himself even he can’t fully see.

The Son of Man

René Magritte · 1964

Magical Realism

Frida Kahlo: Painting Pain

Frida Kahlo didn’t consider herself a Surrealist — she called her work Magical Realism. But both movements share the same spirit: blending the real and the fantastical to reveal a deeper emotional truth.

In The Wounded Deer, a deer’s body carries Frida’s face, pierced by arrows in a dark forest. It sounds impossible. Yet it’s the most accurate portrait ever painted of what chronic pain feels like from the inside.

Frida suffered a devastating bus accident at 18. She painted herself over 55 times — because, she said, she knew herself best.

Self-Portrait with Thorn Necklace and Hummingbird

Frida Kahlo · 1940 · University of Texas

Movement IV

Cubism: Every Angle at Once

Cubism’s question was radical: why show only one perspective? A photograph is always taken from one fixed point of view. Picasso and Braque shattered that limitation — showing a face from the front and in profile simultaneously, in the same canvas.

Les Demoiselles d’Avignon

Pablo Picasso · 1907 · MoMA, New York

Look at this cubist painting: where are they looking? It’s impossible to tell. That’s the point. Picasso shows several angles at once — front, side, three-quarter — all collapsed into a single image.

The result is disorienting, fragmented, alive. No camera could ever do this. A photograph freezes one instant from one point of view. Cubism gives you all instants, all views, at once.

Picasso was a deep student of classical tradition — he painted mythological themes like the Judgment of Paris and religious subjects like crucifixions, but always through his own revolutionary lens.

Guernica

Pablo Picasso · 1937 · Museo Reina Sofía, Madrid

In 1937, Nazi warplanes bombed the Basque town of Guernica in northern Spain. Thousands of civilians died. Picasso responded with the most powerful anti-war statement in art history.

The painting is entirely in black, white, and grey — like a newspaper photograph, like a bombed-out world drained of color. It’s fragmented, chaotic, full of screaming figures, a dying horse, a mother holding her dead child.

Cubism here isn’t a style game — it’s the language of destruction. Bodies broken into fragments, reality shattered like the town itself. You don’t just see the horror; you feel it dismantling you.

Many consider Guernica the most important painting of the 20th century — not because of its technique, but because of what it does to you.

History Never Ends

The Old Stories Return

Even in the most revolutionary era of art, painters kept returning to the themes we’ve followed throughout this journey: Judith, the Judgment of Paris, the Bible, mythology.

Judith I

Gustav Klimt · 1901 · Österreichische Galerie Belvedere

Gustav Klimt, the great Art Nouveau master, painted Judith — the same biblical heroine we traced from Botticelli to Goya — but now draped in gold mosaic patterns, half-clothed, her expression sensual and triumphant.

The golden frame is part of the painting. Holofernes’ severed head barely visible at the bottom. Judith looks almost bored — as if this power is simply natural to her.

A theme from the 6th century BCE, painted in 1901, still electric. Some stories never lose their power.

The Twist

Art History Is Not Linear

Here’s a painting from 1500. It looks like it could be a 20th-century surrealist work. The figures are distorted, dreamlike, grotesque. Impossible creatures fill the frame.

It’s The Garden of Earthly Delights by Hieronymus Bosch — painted 400 years before Dalí was born.

Humans are unpredictable. Great art breaks timelines. A medieval illuminator might paint abstract shapes. A Baroque artist might feel like an Expressionist. The movements we study are guides, not walls.

The Garden of Earthly Delights (detail)

Hieronymus Bosch · c. 1500-1510 · Museo del Prado, Madrid

The Final Frontier

Postmodernism: What Even Is Art?

In 1917, Marcel Duchamp took a urinal, signed it “R. Mutt,” and submitted it to an art exhibition. It was rejected. But the question it raised has never been answered:

If this painting is art, and this sculpture is art — why isn’t a urinal? Who decides? Who draws the line?

Postmodern art is art about art — questioning what it means to call something valuable, beautiful, or worthy of attention. It doesn’t give answers. It gives questions.

Campbell’s Soup Cans

Andy Warhol · 1962 · MoMA, New York

Andy Warhol placed 32 identical soup can paintings side by side like supermarket shelves. You might laugh. You might shrug. But Warhol forces you to ask:

“Why do I value a Rembrandt and not a soup can?”

The answer reveals more about you than about either painting.

With the 20th century, we close our journey through art history.

From Greek temples to melting clocks. From Roman mosaics to Campbell’s soup cans. From Botticelli’s golden Judith to Klimt’s golden Judith. Art doesn’t end — it transforms.

Every movement we explored was once radical, dangerous, misunderstood. Monet was rejected. Van Gogh sold one painting in his life. Picasso was called a charlatan. Today they hang in museums and live inside every artist who came after.
