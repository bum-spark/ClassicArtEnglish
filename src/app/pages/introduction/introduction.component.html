Introduction to
Art History

Understanding how time works in art history, and why the Classical Era shaped everything that came after.

The Timeline

Understanding Time

Year 1 is considered the year when Jesus Christ was born. Before that, we count backwards and call them years before Christ (BC). If we look both behind and ahead, we find the Ancient Roman Empire — think of Julius Caesar. And before Rome, there was Ancient Greece.

Together, these two civilizations form what we call the Classical Era. The word "classic" means something considered the best in its category, a reference point. And the influence they left us is enormous — our languages come from Latin, our calendar from Rome, and our philosophy from Greek thinkers like Plato, Aristotle, Euclid, and Pythagoras.

800 BC — 476 AD

The Classical Era

Ancient Greece gave us myths like the Trojan War and Hercules, great mathematicians like Euclid and Pythagoras, and philosophers like Plato and Aristotle who shaped how we think even today.

You have probably already seen classical art without knowing it — The Judgment of Paris, Greek ceramics (like in the Hercules movie), Pompeiian frescoes, or the mighty Colosseum.

Mythology Philosophy Sculpture

500 — 1400

The Middle Ages

Christianity became an enormous influence on art. This is the era of the typical paintings of Jesus Christ, the Virgin Mary, and the Three Wise Men.

During this period, Christian art expanded and two major styles developed: Romanesque and Gothic art and architecture.

Christianity Romanesque Gothic

1400 — 1800

Renaissance, Baroque
& Neoclassicism

Neoclassicism means "new classicism" — artists went back to being inspired by Greece and Rome. For example, Botticelli's Venus is based on the Greco-Roman goddess.

But they also kept representing Christian themes, like Leonardo da Vinci's The Last Supper, showing Jesus before being captured.

Artists became fascinated with drawing things as they really are. They learned about proportions, started using conic perspective — a technique to represent 3D space on a flat surface — and mastered light and shadow.

Perspective Proportion Light

1800 — 1899

The 19th Century

Political changes like the Industrial Revolution transformed society. Artists started developing as individuals, no longer working exclusively for kings or the church.

Movements like Romanticism, Impressionism, and Realism emerged. Brushstrokes became freer — Monet's Impression, Sunrise gave its name to an entire movement.

Artists stopped chasing photorealism and started playing with color, like Van Gogh's The Starry Night.

Impressionism Romanticism Realism

1900 — 1999

The 20th Century

The camera changed everything. Before, when a king wanted a portrait, he needed a painter to replicate reality as faithfully as possible. One of the artist's main functions was purely practical — make the artichoke look like an artichoke, make the person look like the person.

With photography, artists were free to explore other things. Expressionism emerged — think of The Scream by Munch. It's not a photo-portrait; you feel the anxiety. Surrealism created dreamlike images that no camera could capture.

Then came Postmodern Art, questioning the very definition of art. Marcel Duchamp signed a urinal and called it Fountain. Is that art? What is art?

2000 — Today

The Present Day

Today we have 3D worlds, artificial intelligence that can generate images of anything you want in any style you want. These are images invented in the moment. And then there are NFTs — digital art with ownership.

Art continues to evolve, but the core remains the same: humans expressing themselves in ways that connect with others.
