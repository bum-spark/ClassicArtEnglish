The
Renaissance

The ancient Greek and Roman texts go viral, classical beauty is reborn, and art discovers oil paint, perspective, and the human body.

The 15th Century

Classical Texts Go Viral

Before the printing press, every book had to be copied by hand, letter by letter. Want a copy? Come back in two years. By then, you probably didn’t care anymore.

But in the 15th century, the printing press arrived. Combined with cheap paper, information could now spread faster than ever. And which texts went viral? The Greek and Roman classics.

People read them and were amazed: “Look at what our Roman ancestors did!” A rebirth of classical culture began — and that’s exactly what the word Renaissance means.

The Birth of Venus

Sandro Botticelli · c. 1485 · Uffizi Gallery, Florence

Botticelli

Venus Reborn

The Birth of Venus is one of the most iconic paintings of the Renaissance. Venus — the Roman goddess of love (Aphrodite in Greek) — is the one who won the Judgment of Paris. She was born from sea foam, and artists often show her standing on a shell.

Her pose, modestly covering herself, comes directly from ancient Greek sculpture — 1,500 years earlier. Even paintings buried under Pompeya’s volcano showed Venus on a shell, just like Botticelli does.

But here’s the key: Botticelli paints a Greco-Roman goddess life-size, on a canvas several meters wide. That simply didn’t happen in the Middle Ages. This is the Renaissance in one painting.

Venus de’ Medici

Greek sculpture · 1st century BC

Pompeii

Same Pose, 1,500 Years Apart

This Roman fresco from Pompeii already shows Venus on a shell, modestly posed. Botticelli didn’t invent this image — he revived it. That’s why we call this era a rebirth.

Leonardo da Vinci

The Most Famous Painting in the World

La Gioconda (Mona Lisa)

Leonardo da Vinci · c. 1503–1519 · Louvre, Paris

Meet Lisa

The first thing you notice is the cracked surface — five centuries of aging. Yet through the cracks, you can see something remarkable: no hard outlines, no black lines defining her eyes. Everything is built with light and shadow alone.

Just a Regular Person

Lisa was nobody famous — just an Italian merchant’s wife. In Italian, “Mrs. Lisa” becomes Madonna Elisa, shortened to Mona Lisa. What matters is not who she is, but how she was painted.

The Magic Background

Behind Lisa: a dreamy landscape painted in reddish tones at the bottom, shifting to blue as it rises — a technique that mimics how real atmosphere works. You can spot a small path, a lake, and a Roman bridge built with arches.

No Eyelashes?

If you look closely, Lisa has no eyebrows or eyelashes. They were once there, but centuries of cleaning and aging erased them. Her transparent veil, the intricate embroidery on her dress, the way light folds in her sleeves — these details made this the most famous painting ever made.

Technology

Eggs, Oil, and Revolution

The Renaissance popularized a revolutionary technique: oil painting. Before this, artists mostly used tempera or fresco. Each had serious limitations.

🏛️

Fresco

Spread wet plaster on a wall, then paint before it dries. The pigment bonds to the wall. Fast, permanent — but you have to work quick and you’re stuck on walls.

🥚

Tempera

Mix pigment with egg yolk, dip your brush, paint. It dries super fast. Layers stack on top of each other without blending. Hard to create soft transitions.

Game-changer
🎨

Oil Paint

Mix pigment with oil (that’s what óleo means). It dries very slowly, so layers blend together. This let Leonardo blur edges and create the Mona Lisa’s famously soft face. From now on, oil dominates.

Raphael

A Question of Perspective

The School of Athens

Raphael Sanzio · 1509–1511 · Apostolic Palace, Vatican

One Vanishing Point, Infinite Depth

The Renaissance invented linear perspective: set a single vanishing point, draw guide lines, and the entire space obeys mathematical rules. Objects get smaller as they move away. The result looks like a 3D video game — you feel like you could walk into it.

Compare this with a Gothic painting from 200 years earlier: columns are paper-thin, the roof doesn’t make sense, proportions are guessed. The Renaissance replaced intuition with mathematics.

A Who’s Who of Ancient Greece

The School of Athens is basically a fan poster of Greek philosophy. Plato, Aristotle, Pythagoras, Euclid — all gathered in one painting, even though they lived centuries apart.

It’s like someone in the year 7000 painting Mozart, John Lennon, and Beyoncé together in one room and calling it “Music.” Yes — that’s the concept, but they never met. That’s how obsessed the Renaissance was with antiquity.

Michelangelo

You Know More About the Sistine Chapel
Than You Think

The Creation of Adam

Michelangelo · c. 1508–1512 · Sistine Chapel, Vatican

Painted Looking Up

The entire Sistine Chapel ceiling was painted by Michelangelo — looking upward. The ceiling tells the story of creation: God separating light from darkness, creating the sun and moon, separating earth from water, creating Adam, creating Eve, the forbidden fruit, the expulsion from Paradise…

And in one corner? Judith and Holofernes — the same story from our Introduction. See? You’re already familiar with the Sistine Chapel.

The Perfect Icon

Michelangelo was the rock star of his time. People watched him paint like fans at a concert. But he was primarily a sculptor — and when he painted the ceiling, he transferred his sculptural thinking to paint.

The Creation of Adam captures one magical moment: God’s finger nearly touching Adam’s, giving him life. The two figures mirror each other — “God created man in His own image” expressed through parallel forms. Just fingertips, almost touching. That’s genius.
