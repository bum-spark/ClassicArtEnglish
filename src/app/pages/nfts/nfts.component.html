The Future · Part II

NFTs
& Digital Ownership

When anyone can copy a file, who truly “owns” a work of art? The answer is older than you think.

The Problem

Original vs. Copy

Mona Lisa

Leonardo da Vinci · c. 1503 · Louvre Museum, Paris

With a physical painting like the Mona Lisa, ownership is simple: whoever holds the canvas in the Louvre owns the original. You can take a photo of it, buy a poster — but the original stays in Paris.

But what about digital art? When you create a drawing on a computer and send someone a copy, both files are identical. There’s no “original” and no “copy” — they’re the same thing.

So how do you prove you “own” a digital artwork when anyone can download the exact same file?

Enter the NFT

An NFT (Non-Fungible Token) is a digital certificate of ownership that lives on a blockchain — the same technology behind Bitcoin and Ethereum.

The artwork itself? Anyone can copy it. Download it, share it, print it, set it as your wallpaper. But the certificate — the proof that says “this person owns it” — cannot be copied or forged. It’s unique and immutable.

Think of it like a deed to a house: you can photograph the house, visit it, admire it. But the deed says who owns it.

This Is Not New

A Banana Worth $120,000

In 2019, the Italian artist Maurizio Cattelan taped a banana to a gallery wall and called it Comedian. Two editions sold for $120,000 each.

But wait — bananas rot in a week. So what did the buyer actually purchase? Not the banana. Cattelan sold a certificate of authenticity and instructions: “Tape a fresh banana to the wall. Replace it every 7–10 days.”

Sound absurd? Perhaps. But it proves a crucial point: separating ownership from the physical object is not a crypto invention — it’s an art world invention. Remember Duchamp’s urinal? Same logic, different century.

“You don’t buy the banana. You buy the idea.”

Today, digital artists like Beeple sell NFTs for millions. You might think it’s silly — just like people thought a banana on a wall was silly, or a urinal in a museum was silly.

You have every right to your opinion. But understanding how it works means you understand where art is heading — and that’s always been this site’s purpose.
