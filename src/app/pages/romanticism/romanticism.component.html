The 19th Century · Part I

Romanticism

When artists stopped following rules, turned their backs to the viewer, and painted the darkness inside their own minds.

Caspar David Friedrich

Turning Their Backs

In the 19th century, three major art movements appear: Romanticism, Realism, and Impressionism.

Friedrich’s Wanderer Above the Sea of Fog is the first painting in our course where the main figure has their back turned to us. And it’s not the only one — Friedrich does this again and again.

His landscapes are often desolate and melancholic. Even when he paints a crucifixion, the landscape matters more than Jesus himself — who is also facing away. Nature becomes a character that communicates mood. That’s very Romantic.

Wanderer Above the Sea of Fog

Caspar David Friedrich · c. 1818 · Kunsthalle Hamburg

The Monk by the Sea

1810 · Alte Nationalgalerie, Berlin

A tiny human surrounded by an enormous, empty sky. The solitude is almost overwhelming.

The Cross in the Mountains

1808 · Galerie Neue Meister, Dresden

Even in a crucifixion scene, the mountain landscape is the true protagonist.

Two Men Contemplating the Moon

c. 1825–1830 · Metropolitan Museum, New York

Again: backs turned, gazing into the vast unknown. That’s Friedrich’s signature.

Thomas Cole

Everything Dies

Thomas Cole painted five canvases telling the story of ancient Rome — from its savage beginnings to the ruins we know today. Look for the mountain with the rock on top: it appears in every single painting, unchanged, while civilization rises and crumbles around it.

I. The Savage State

Primitive beginnings

II. The Pastoral State

Civilization begins

III. The Consummation

Peak glory

IV. Destruction

Barbarian invasions

V. Desolation

Ruins remain

Cole isn’t really talking about Rome. He’s talking about death — about the idea that everything ends.

One day you will die. Your memory will eventually disappear, just like Rome’s glory. These dark themes fascinated the Romantics — an entire movement built on the most intense, sublime, and terrifying human emotions.

A Revolution

Art Breaks Free

Something fundamental changed in this era. The French Revolution and American Independence shifted who holds power — and who pays for art.

Friedrich didn’t paint on commission. He painted at home, whatever he wanted, and then sold to whoever liked it. That’s a massive change from Velázquez, who lived in the king’s palace and painted what the king told him to.

Romantics didn’t like Neoclassicism’s coldness. Sure, technically flawless — but lifeless figures with no emotion. They wanted melancholy, solitude, and fear. Look at the empty space in these paintings: a king from past centuries would have complained about paying for half-empty canvases.

👑

Before: Royal Patronage

The king hired you, fed you, gave you materials. In return, you painted exactly what he wanted — portraits, battles, religious scenes.

🎨

After: The Artist as Individual

After the revolutions, artists express their own emotions. Friedrich paints desolate landscapes because he wants to — then whoever likes it can buy it.

Francisco de Goya

Goya Went Dark

The Parasol

Francisco de Goya · 1777 · Museo del Prado

Goya is a fascinating character. His early work is bright, colorful, and Neoclassical — cheerful scenes like The Parasol.

But over time, his art becomes increasingly dark. His most famous late works are the Black Paintings — a series he painted directly on the walls of his own house.

Not on canvas. Not for a patron. He literally came home and saw these nightmares on his walls. The themes? Death, destruction, fear, demons. Pure Romanticism.

Saturn Devouring His Son

c. 1820–1823 · Museo del Prado

This was in the dining room. Imagine sitting down to eat with this staring at you. It shows the god Saturn from Greek mythology — a classical theme, but now painted with Romantic horror.

Witches’ Sabbath (The Great He-Goat)

c. 1821–1823 · Museo del Prado

A gathering of witches. The goat figure is the devil. Dark, terrifying, and deeply personal — this was Goya’s private nightmare.

Atropos (The Fates)

The three Fates from Greek myth who control every mortal’s thread of life. When they cut it — you die. Another classical theme turned dark.

The Dog

A dog drowning in mud — or sand, or emptiness. One of the most suffocating images in art history.

Fight with Cudgels

Two men beating each other with clubs, blood on their faces. Raw, violent, primal — pure Romantic darkness.

The Sleep of Reason Produces Monsters

1799 · Los Caprichos, No. 43

When Reason Sleeps

This etching is perhaps Goya’s most iconic work. A figure slumps over a desk, asleep, while bats, owls, and deformed creatures swarm out of the darkness behind him.

The creatures don’t come from outside — they come from his own mind. When reason goes to sleep, the monsters wake up.

Goya is no longer obsessed with anatomical perfection. He’s flowing with his own obsessions, exploring the darkest corners of the human psyche. That’s Romanticism at its core.
