The Key Difference

Light Was Born
(And So Was Shadow)

The fastest way to tell Renaissance from Baroque? Look at the light.

In Raphael’s School of Athens, light pours in from everywhere — every figure is evenly lit, colors are vivid, nothing hides in shadow. It’s like a sunny day with no clouds.

Now look at Las Meninas by Velázquez: the painting is brown and black. Half the scene sits in darkness. A window on the right lets light hit the foreground figures, while the entire back wall disappears into shadow. This is the Baroque revolution: dramatic, aggressive lighting.

Baroque · Dramatic shadow · Velázquez

Artemisia Gentileschi

Judith Returns — Darker Than Ever

Remember Judith and Holofernes from the Introduction? In the Baroque, Artemisia Gentileschi paints the same story — but with extreme contrast between light and shadow. The result is visceral.

Judith: Renaissance vs. Baroque

Botticelli (Renaissance) — Judith looks like she’s going on a picnic. Bright light, every detail visible. The scene is calm and serene.

Artemisia (Baroque) — Half of Judith’s face is hidden by shadow. The lighting is so extreme it’s almost violent. That’s the Baroque.

Velázquez

Unpacking Las Meninas

Las Meninas

Diego Velázquez · 1656 · Museo del Prado, Madrid

Only Possible After the Renaissance

Las Meninas uses oil paint (from the Renaissance) to blur edges without hard outlines — just like the Mona Lisa. It uses linear perspective (also from the Renaissance) to create a believable room. But the dramatic window-light illumination? That’s pure Baroque.

The Room Is the Star

Most of the canvas is taken up by a room. Paintings on the back wall are deliberately blurred — because they’re far away, and in a dark room, distant things look fuzzy. A Renaissance painter would have drawn them in perfect detail because he knows they’re there. Velázquez paints what you see, not what you know.

A Painting Inside a Painting

A huge chunk of the canvas shows the back of another canvas. It’s meta-painting: art about art. Velázquez even paints himself standing next to the Infanta (the king’s daughter) and the reflected image of King Philip IV. The king letting the painter stand beside royalty? That’s how much Philip valued him.

Velázquez vs. the Others

Compare three portraits of Philip IV from the same decade by three different painters. Velázquez’s version has shadows under the nose, a slight gleam on the lip, three-dimensional cheekbones. The others? Flat, ivory-colored skin, zero depth. It’s clear why the king kept Velázquez close.

The Name

Why “Baroque” Was an Insult

The word Baroque comes from baroque pearls — irregular, asymmetric pearls that were the opposite of the perfect spherical ones.

Calling someone “Baroque” was like saying “you’re deformed and exaggerated.” The Renaissance was the perfect sphere; the Baroque was the weird, asymmetric pearl.

But that “weirdness” is exactly what makes Baroque art so powerful: more expressive, more dramatic, more human.

Rubens

God’s Light Is About 100 Watts

The Adoration of the Magi

Peter Paul Rubens · 1609–1629 · Museo del Prado, Madrid

An Avalanche of People

In Raphael’s Renaissance, everyone stands still like models. In Rubens’s painting, people rush toward baby Jesus like fans at a concert. They’re crammed together — even the horse wants a look. The space is absurd, but the storytelling is brilliant.

And the light source? Baby Jesus himself glows. Everyone around him is lit from that single source, while the rest fades into darkness. Baroque lighting at its finest.

Classical Ruins on Purpose

Look carefully: behind the crowd, Rubens placed a Greco-Roman column from some ancient ruin, with a spider web attached. Even when painting a Christian scene, the Baroque artists couldn’t resist a classical reference.

The faces are incredibly expressive — furrowed brows, raised eyebrows, mouths about to speak. These are the same kinds of intense faces that Dutch painters were exploring in their tronies.

Vermeer

The Girl with a Pearl Earring

Girl with a Pearl Earring

Johannes Vermeer · c. 1665 · Mauritshuis, The Hague

No Outlines, Just Light

Like the Mona Lisa, there are no hard lines defining this face — only light and shadow. But while Lisa looks like she’s been posing for 18 hours, this girl looks like she just turned around and said “What?”

It feels like a candid photo. That naturalness is what makes Vermeer special. Compare her face to other portraits of the era: their skin looks like ivory, their hair is stiff. Vermeer’s girl feels alive.

This painting is a tronie — a type of Baroque Dutch painting featuring expressive faces and exotic costumes. It’s possibly the most famous tronie ever.
