The 19th Century · Part III

Van Gogh

When colors stopped being real and started being emotions — the painter who invented his own universe.

Post-Impressionism

Van Gogh Invents Colors

The Impressionist style — loose brushstrokes, light over detail — opened a door. Post-Impressionism walked through it and took everything to the extreme. Its greatest representative? Vincent van Gogh.

Look at The Sower: a man walking through grass, a row of wheat behind him, a house in the corner, and a huge sun rising in the center. The figures are painted with broad, rough brushstrokes — like Impressionism.

But here’s the twist: Van Gogh doesn’t want realistic color. His plants are blue. The house is blue. The trees are blue. And the sun is so enormous its rays become the entire sky — turning it green and yellow.

The sky has green. The plants have blue. Colors are inverted. That’s not a mistake — that’s Van Gogh’s entire philosophy.

The Sower

Vincent van Gogh · 1888 · Van Gogh Museum, Amsterdam

Self-Portrait

1889 · Musée d’Orsay, Paris

Painting the Infinite

Look at this self-portrait: green eyebrows, dark red shadows on his eyelids, pink-orange-green hair. The background isn’t a room — it’s a swirling ether of energy.

Remember Girl with a Pearl Earring? Vermeer also used a plain dark background. But Van Gogh said: “If I’m going to paint a background, I’ll make it magical.”

“Behind the head, instead of a common room wall, I paint the infinite.” — Van Gogh, in a letter describing another portrait

The Masterpiece

The Starry Night

The Starry Night

Vincent van Gogh · 1889 · MoMA, New York

This is probably the most famous painting by Van Gogh, and one of the most recognized works of art in the world.

The sky is alive with enormous swirling spirals of light. Stars don’t twinkle — they explode. The moon radiates energy. A dark cypress tree in the foreground reaches up like a flame toward the sky.

Below, a quiet little village sleeps peacefully, unaware of the cosmic drama happening above. It’s nature as an emotional character — just like Friedrich did in Romanticism, but pushed to a whole new level.

Van Gogh painted this from the window of a psychiatric hospital in Saint-Rémy-de-Provence, France. He was going through one of the hardest periods of his life.

But instead of painting what he literally saw, he painted what he felt: a sky pulsating with energy, light that moves and breathes and doesn’t stay still. The brushstrokes are thick, visible, almost desperate — each one carrying emotion.

The sky in this painting is not the sky. It’s Van Gogh’s inner world projected onto the canvas.

A Returning Tradition

The Still Life Strikes Back

Remember the ancient Roman still life buried by a volcano? And Clara Peeters painting her own reflection in metal cups? Van Gogh continues this tradition with the most famous flower paintings in history.

Sunflowers

Vincent van Gogh · 1888 · National Gallery, London

Yellow, green, orange, even blue sunflowers. Van Gogh imitates the real texture of a sunflower by sculpting with paint — thick blobs of pigment that rise off the canvas. The background is yellow. The vase is yellow. The table is yellow. Everything responds to the sunflowers.

Still-Life with Flowers and Plums

Rachel Ruysch · c. 1680s · Baroque

A Baroque still life: every leaf, every petal, every shadow rendered with scientific precision. You could identify each plant’s Latin name. The dark background is a neutral ether — just like Vermeer’s. Beautiful, but clinical.

Ruysch’s Baroque still life is almost a scientific catalogue — every flower faithfully reproduced. Van Gogh’s sunflowers are more schematic — sometimes flat, sometimes sculpted with thick paint.

But that background! Ruysch uses a dark void. Van Gogh uses yellow — the same color as the flowers, as the vase, as the table. Everything vibrates together, creating an energy that a photograph could never capture.

The Big Picture

Every Tradition Lives On

Van Gogh doesn’t just belong to Post-Impressionism. He carries every movement of the 19th century inside his work.

🌊

Like the Romantics

Friedrich treated nature as a character that transmits emotions. Van Gogh does the same in The Starry Night — nature is sublime, overwhelming, alive.

🌾

Like the Realists

Realists painted everyday scenes — farmers, workers, daily life. Van Gogh paints a farmer going to work and a boy reaping wheat. Real life, simple subjects.

☀️

Like the Impressionists

Monet painted haystacks over and over to explore how light changes. Van Gogh paints sunflowers over and over to explore how color can make them more alive.

✝️

The Christian Tradition

Van Gogh also continues religious art. He paints a Bible still life and a Pietà — the Virgin holding dead Jesus — but in Post-Impressionist colors.

Pietà (after Delacroix)

Vincent van Gogh · 1889 · Van Gogh Museum

Jesus’s skin is blue, green, yellow, pink. The rock is painted in blues, browns, and greens. The canvas shows through. Yet if you say “Post-Impressionist Pietà” — you understand it perfectly.

Still Life with Bible

Vincent van Gogh · 1885 · Van Gogh Museum

A Bible and a candle — the Christian still-life tradition that has existed since the earliest days of art, now rendered through Van Gogh’s unique lens.

With Van Gogh, we close the 19th century.

If you look at the themes he painted, they connect to every movement of his era: Romantic nature, Realist everyday life, Impressionist repetition — and the Christian tradition that has run through art for over a thousand years.

Next comes the 20th century: cameras, wars, atomic bombs, and dozens of new movements — Cubism, Expressionism, Surrealism. Names like Picasso, Dalí, Frida Kahlo, and Kandinsky await.
