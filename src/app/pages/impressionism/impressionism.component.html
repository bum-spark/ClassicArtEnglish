Before Impressionism

Realism: Enough with Gods

While Romantics explored dark versions of classical myths, another group said: “Enough with myths entirely. What about real people?”

Realism represents everyday life in a believable way. The word “realism” doesn’t mean the technique is more realistic — it means the themes are real. No more gods. No more myths. Just women harvesting wheat, people on a train, or a girl praying at night.

These are costumbrista scenes — slices of everyday life that nobody had bothered to paint before.

A Burial at Ornans

A regular funeral in a small town. Courbet gave everyday people the same grandeur previously reserved for kings and saints.

A Technology Revolution

The Paint Tube Changed Everything

One day, Monet shows a painting called Impression, Sunrise — and from that word, “Impressionism” is born.

These painters grab their easel, their paints, and go outside to paint. Street dances, a girl in a boat, ducks on a pond. But why didn’t anyone do this before?

Because in Rubens’s time, pigments had to be ground by hand and mixed with oil right then and there, or they’d oxidize. If Rubens wanted to paint poppies in a field, he’d need a whole procession of helpers carrying supplies. It was a circus.

Now? Mass-produced paint tubes and cheap factory canvases. Going outside to paint became trivial. And that changed everything.

Impression, Sunrise

Claude Monet · 1872 · Musée Marmottan Monet, Paris

🎨

Before: A Whole Production

Pigments ground by hand, mixed with oil on the spot. Painting outdoors meant bringing an entire team of assistants. Most painting happened inside studios.

🏭

After: Just Grab and Go

Factory-made paint tubes + affordable canvases = total freedom. Artists could go to a park, a riverside, or a street dance and paint whatever they saw.

Claude Monet

The True Protagonist Is Light

Woman with a Parasol

Claude Monet · 1875 · National Gallery, Washington D.C.

Compare an Impressionist figure with a Rafael painting. Rafael seems “more realistic” because he painted every eyelash, every wrinkle. But blur both images and look only at the light — Monet’s version could be a real blurred photograph.

This woman is backlit (the sun is behind her). Monet paints her with very dark tones — which feels counter-intuitive for a sunny day in the countryside.

But that’s exactly how your eyes work. When you look toward bright light, everything in front of it turns dark. It’s a concept called exposure — and Monet was one of the first artists to truly understand it.

La Grenouillère

Claude Monet · 1869 · Metropolitan Museum of Art

Look at the water. Depending on how light bounces off it, Monet paints it white or black, with blue reflections from the sky, green reflections from the trees, dark vertical reflections of people, and the water’s own ripples.

The people aren’t painted with detail because they aren’t the protagonist. The water is. The light is.

And Monet’s obsession didn’t stop at water. He would set up in a field and paint the same haystacks over and over — at dawn, at dusk, at noon, on cloudy days, on clear days.

He was studying how the exact same object changes before our eyes when the light changes. It’s almost a scientific experiment — repeated observations of the same subject to find the tiny differences.

Light Everywhere

Seeing What Nobody Noticed

The Floor Scrapers

Gustave Caillebotte · 1875 · Musée d’Orsay

Why would anyone paint men scraping a floor? Because Caillebotte noticed something fascinating: the polished parts of the floor reflect light differently from the rough parts. That tiny difference in how light bounces off a surface is enough to make an entire painting.

Dance at Le Moulin de la Galette

Pierre-Auguste Renoir · 1876 · Musée d’Orsay

A street dance on a Sunday afternoon. Look at the dappled sunlight filtering through the trees, creating spots of light and shadow on the dancers. The real subject is how light plays across a crowd.

Beyond Mythology

New Protagonists

Impressionism didn’t just change technique — it changed who gets painted. Compare two women painters from two different eras, and the shift becomes obvious.

Venus Induces Helen to Fall in Love with Paris

Angelica Kauffmann · 1790 · Neoclassical

Kauffmann paints stories from Greek mythology. Venus, Helen, Paris — grand epic characters from ancient literature.

The Boating Party

Mary Cassatt · 1893–1894 · Impressionist

Cassatt paints her real life — mothers with children, bored girls at home, a sunny afternoon on a boat. No gods. No myths. Just people.

“In many Impressionist paintings, the true protagonist isn’t a person — it’s the light itself.”
