The Long Neoclassicism · Part III

Neoclassicism

The pendulum swings back to perfect proportions, serene beauty, and an obsession with Greece and Rome that goes off the charts.

The Pattern

Art History Is a Pendulum

By now, you might sense a pattern. In the Renaissance, artists took a very intellectual, mathematical approach: serene expressions, ideal beauty, the perfect spherical pearl.

In the Baroque, they broke free: more movement, more shadow, more emotion — the irregular pearl.

Now in Neoclassicism, the pendulum swings back: they want everything perfect, balanced, calm, and in its place. Back to the spherical pearl.

Jacques-Louis David

A Battle Where Nobody Sweats

Leonidas at Thermopylae

Jacques-Louis David · 1814 · Louvre, Paris

This painting shows the Battle of Thermopylae — the same story as the movie 300. Spartans about to die fighting the Persians, sacrificing themselves for Greece.

But look at them: nobody sweats, nobody screams, nobody has messy hair. They look like they’re at a fashion show. It’s anatomically perfect — but it feels like the people have no blood in their veins.

That’s the Neoclassical paradox: everything is so perfect that it stops feeling human. The proportions are impeccable, the anatomy flawless, but the emotion? Gone.

Compare this to Artemisia’s Judith — messy, violent, half-hidden in shadow. The Baroque made you feel something. Neoclassicism makes you admire something.

Angelica Kauffmann

From Paris to Darth Vader

Neoclassical artists were obsessed with Greek and Roman mythology. The printing press was well-established, books were cheaper, and they had read every myth. But they went further: they started painting the in-between moments.

Remember the Judgment of Paris? Venus promised Paris the love of Helen. Well — at some point, Venus had to introduce Helen to Paris, right? Angelica Kauffmann painted exactly that moment.

It’s like Star Wars: the original movie starts with Darth Vader walking through a door. Then 40 years later, Disney makes a whole film about what Vader was doing 10 minutes before walking through that door. That’s Neoclassicism — mythology fan fiction.

Venus Induces Helen to Fall in Love with Paris

Angelica Kauffmann · 1790

An Old Friend

Zeuxis Paints Helen

Kauffmann also painted Zeuxis — the Greek painter from our Introduction (the one with the grapes that fooled birds). Here he’s selecting models to paint Helen of Troy, the most beautiful mortal on Earth.

This is how deep the Neoclassical fandom went: they painted a Greek artist painting a Greek myth. Meta-classical.

Architecture

You Already Know
Neoclassical Art

After the French Revolution in 1789, Neoclassicism became almost an official style. And when a brand-new country called the United States needed buildings and monuments, guess what style they chose?

The White House

Greco-Roman columns, a triangular pediment on top — just like the Parthenon. But it’s white and smooth, imitating the ruins as they looked in the 18th century, not the colorful originals. That’s why they could call it “The White House.”

The Capitol

Also Greco-Roman inspired. This one even has sculptures in the pediment, just like the original Greek temples. The dome echoes the Roman Pantheon.

The Statue of Liberty

Built about 100 years after the White House, but still Neoclassical in spirit. She represents Libertas, the Roman goddess of freedom.

She’s green because she’s made of copper that has oxidized over time. She was originally brown. And just like ancient bronze statues that lost their paint centuries ago, nobody repainted her either.

The Big Reveal

All Roads Lead to Rome

Here’s something mind-blowing: every art period we’ve studied gets its name from how it relates to ancient Greece and Rome. Either it imitates the classics, or it’s an insult for not imitating them.

🏛️

Romanesque = “like Rome”

Their arches copied Roman semicircular arches. Pro-classical. ✓

⚔️

Gothic = “barbaric” (insult)

Named after the Goths who invaded Rome. Pointed arches replaced round ones. Anti-classical. ✗

🌅

Renaissance = “rebirth”

The rebirth of Greco-Roman culture. Pro-classical. ✓

🫧

Baroque = “deformed pearl” (insult)

Named after irregular pearls. Too exaggerated compared to classical perfection. Anti-classical. ✗

⚪

Neoclassicism = “the new classical”

Classical ideals taken to the extreme. Maximum pro-classical. ✓✓

📍

Middle Ages = “the thing in the middle” (insult)

Just the stuff between classical antiquity and the next classical revival. Even the name is dismissive.

“Every period is defined by how it relates to Greece and Rome — by imitation or by opposition.”

This is why we called this macro-period the “Long Neoclassicism”
