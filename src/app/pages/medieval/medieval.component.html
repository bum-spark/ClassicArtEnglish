Rome falls. The Church rises. And art becomes a Bible for those who cannot read.

The Collapse

Barbarians at the Gates

The Western Roman Empire didn’t fall like a building. It crumbled, slowly, over many years. The Empire could no longer defend its borders, and wave after wave of invasions came from peoples the Romans called “barbarians.”

By this point, Christianity was already the official religion. So as the Emperor’s power faded, the Church stepped in to fill the vacuum.

The Pope — inspired by the Roman Pontifex Maximus — became a political leader, not just a religious one. In many ways, the Church became the natural heir of the Roman Empire.

The Empire Crumbles

Invasions, weak borders, economic collapse. Rome loses control of Western Europe.

The Church Steps In

Christianity was already official. When Rome fell, the Pope filled the power gap.

Art Follows the Money

The Church had money, so it paid for art. That’s why most medieval art tells Bible stories.

The Source Material

The Bible — A Quick Guide

To understand medieval art, you need to know the book that inspired almost all of it.

Part One

Old Testament

The older and larger part. It contains the ancient stories you’ve probably heard:

  • • The Creation of Adam and Eve
  • • Noah’s Ark and the great flood
  • • Moses and the Ten Commandments
  • • The story of Judith — which we already covered!

Part Two

New Testament

The second part tells the story of Jesus Christ and what happened after him.

A group of Jews believed the promised Messiah had arrived — his name was Jesus. Others disagreed. Those who believed founded Christianity and added this new section to the existing Scriptures.

The Central Figure

Jesus Christ Superstar

His story will become the single most painted subject in Western art for the next 1,500 years.

The Story

Mary, engaged to Joseph, was visited by an angel who told her she would become the mother of the Son of God. They traveled to Bethlehem, where she gave birth to Jesus in a manger — because there was no room at any inn.

When he grew up, Jesus preached about the Kingdom of God. He healed the sick, walked on water, multiplied bread and fish. His followers were fishermen and tax collectors — ordinary people, not philosophers.

The Roman authorities saw him as a threat. He was captured, tortured, crowned with thorns, and forced to carry his own cross. He was crucified. Days later, his followers found the tomb empty. He had risen.

Birth

Born in a manger. Shepherds and the Three Wise Men came to see the newborn.

Miracles

Healed the sick, walked on water, turned water into wine, multiplied bread.

Death & Resurrection

Crucified by the Romans. His tomb was found empty three days later.

Why It Matters for Art

Birth, miracles, suffering, death, resurrection — every chapter of this story will be painted thousands of times for centuries to come.

The Transformation

From Hercules to Jesus

The gods changed, but the art style stayed exactly the same.

Christianity was born inside the Roman Empire. At first, Rome persecuted Christians. But by the 4th century, Emperor Theodosius made Christianity the official religion. From that point on, the same artists who carved Hercules started carving Jesus — using the exact same techniques.

Before

Roman Sarcophagus

Carved like a comic strip in stone. Each panel shows a different labor of Hercules. Roman columns, Roman togas, Roman visual language.

Hercules sarcophagus · c. 3rd century AD

After

Christian Sarcophagus

Same exact style. Same comic-strip format. Same Roman columns. But instead of Hercules, it shows scenes from the life of Jesus.

Sarcophagus of Junius Bassus · 359 AD · Vatican
